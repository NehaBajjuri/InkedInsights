The Streets of India

Ramachandran Clock Wed 02, 2022 12

As one takes a walk through the streets of any Indian city, be it Chennai, Mumbai, Kolkata, Delhi, the trappings of colonial rule are quite conspicuous. The people out at the street are often stereotyped as being shabby, unhygienic, poor, pathetic, illiterate and uncivilized in no unequal terms. They are called the unorganized sector or the “India Uninc”. The so called dazzy, elite, corporate, organized, literate “India Inc” cannot be what it is without the India Uninc. Be it from the delivery of milk to newspapers to vegetables to groceries to roadside eateries to maids to drivers to laundry, a day for the corporates’ can never be complete without the Streetman. The streets of India are bustling with activities day and night, contributing to the growth story of India. To put things in perspective, the unorganized sector contributes to 60% of India’s GDP, 90% of Indian workforce of 500 million are from India uninc. But to understand India, one must understand its streets. For those talking about the business models and innovation models of the unicorns and bluechips, pause a second and think. Can you beat the “India uninc” in any of the benchmarks – be it profitability, innovation, agility, marketing capacity, distribution, distribution of wealth, animal spirits of entrepreneurship. I bet in this game of Inc vs Uninc, the Uninc will win handsdown.

Innovation: Anyone with a deep eye would have noticed that the same vendor out at the street would be selling different goods at different points in the year. It would a Christmas cap, National flag, Holi colours, coloured kites, Diwali crackers. It is to say that the same vendor shifts his business model once in a month to stay put in the market and there at the India Inc, we can hear all management theories which emphasis that it takes years to shift to a new business model. The roles played by small kirana stores (mom and pop shops), small manufacturing units, household enterprises and other such units in the economy of the country is what keeps the cities running (If there is no Dharavi, then there is no Mumbai, Got it!).

Launching of products: Every time you walk through the streets, some new product would have caught your attention. It might be a new toy or car cleaning brush or hair massager. You might not find them when you walk at some other point of the year. The planning of things that go behind this product launch is amazing. The product would have been a Chinese origin, but the timing of product launch, its shipping, marketing and distribution of product is so perfectly done that, the product is launched simultaneously at all shops of the street. This coordination from designing the idea of product to the launch is never accomplished at the Corporate level. Mind you, the streetmen are not B-school passouts!!

Collectiveness: Capitalism is equated to individualistic mindset. Rivals are treated as rivals. Period. At the individual level, organized class may beat the streetmen. But when we look at the collective level, the streetmen will outperform the former. Despite each shop in the street being a rival to one another, we can see a sense of collectiveness among them, a deep bondage between them. Say as you step in a streetshop for any product, but if the product is not available at specific shop, you will be guided by them as to which shop as this product. ( I am sure those who have visited Crawford market, Mumbai will concur this). Have anyone felt the same in any shopping mall. Never. The blunt answer by shopkeeper is “You better search for yourself”. So that’s the kind of collectiveness and fraternity missing in the big India Inc story.

Marketing Skills: Whats unique about say Ranaganthan Street, Chennai; Fashion street, Bangalore; Linking road, Mumbai; Sarojini Market, New Delhi; Bombay market, Surat. All these sell dresses, the trendsetters are often found here and then what? All the shops out there are identical in a sense all will have the same jean or same shirts. Here comes the marketing ability and agility of the streetmen.. Yes right, but the time to market is only say thirty second to one minute because within this time period, if the vendor is not able to convince the customer, he loses the customer. There is no big hoardings to promote Pepe or Levis. Yet the street are jampacked vis-à-vis shopping malls. That’s their USP.

Equal distribution of wealth: Have you ever eaten Idli from street vendor? How much does he charge? Maybe, twenty rupees for a plate of two idlis. Out of the twenty, five rupees will be his income, rest will be distributed equally among the workers who made the dough, who packed the idli, who rode the rickshaw. There is equal distribution of wealth. Now shift your mental frame to having a idli at Taj Connemora, Chennai or Sheraton, Pune. Not only you end up paying five hundred rupees but have you ever wondered out of that five hundred, four hundred will remain with the hotel. Yup! You got it right. There is unequal distribution of wealth. No wonder, the top one percent of India Inc owns sixty percent of India’s wealth. What should we call it? Prosperity? or Prosperity by widening poverty? Think about it.
